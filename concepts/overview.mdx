## Introduction

The `sigmaepsilon.solid.fourier` library provides semi-analytic solutions for beam and plate bending problems using **Navier's method**. This approach leverages Fourier series to satisfy boundary conditions inherently through the choice of approximating functions.

<Info>
While Navier's method is limited to specific boundary conditions, it offers significant computational advantages over numerical methods like FEM when applicable.
</Info>

## What is Navier's Method?

Navier's method solves differential equations by approximating the solution as a **double Fourier series** (for plates) or a **single Fourier series** (for beams):

### For Beams

The transverse displacement is approximated as:

$$
w(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} W_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)
$$

where:
- $W_n$ are the unknown Fourier coefficients
- $N$ is the number of harmonic terms
- $L$ is the beam length

### For Plates

The transverse displacement is approximated as:

$$
w(x, y) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \sum_{n=1}^{N} W_{mn} \sin\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L_x}\right) \sin\left(\frac{n\pi y}{L_y}\right)
$$

where:
- $W_{mn}$ are the unknown Fourier coefficients
- $M$ and $N$ are the numbers of harmonic terms in each direction
- $L_x$ and $L_y$ are the plate dimensions

## Simply-Supported Boundary Conditions

The sine functions used in Navier's method **automatically satisfy simply-supported boundary conditions**:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Zero Displacement" icon="arrows-to-dot">
    At the boundaries, $\sin(0) = \sin(\pi) = 0$, ensuring zero displacement
  </Card>
  <Card title="Zero Bending Moment" icon="wave-sine">
    The second derivative of sine functions also vanishes at boundaries, satisfying zero moment conditions
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

For a simply-supported beam at $x = 0$ and $x = L$:
- $w(0) = w(L) = 0$ (zero displacement)
- $M(0) = M(L) = 0$ (zero bending moment)

For a simply-supported rectangular plate, all four edges satisfy:
- $w = 0$ (zero displacement)
- $M_n = 0$ (zero normal bending moment)

where $M_n$ is the bending moment normal to the edge.

## Load Representation in Fourier Space

Loads are also represented in Fourier space using the same basis functions. For a distributed load $q(x, y)$ on a plate:

$$
q(x, y) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \sum_{n=1}^{N} Q_{mn} \sin\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L_x}\right) \sin\left(\frac{n\pi y}{L_y}\right)
$$

The coefficients $Q_{mn}$ are calculated using orthogonality:

$$
Q_{mn} = \frac{4}{L_x L_y} \int_0^{L_x} \int_0^{L_y} q(x, y) \sin\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L_x}\right) \sin\left(\frac{n\pi y}{L_y}\right) dx dy
$$

The library handles this computation automatically for various load types (point, line, rectangular, disk) and uses Monte-Carlo integration for arbitrary load distributions.

## When to Use Navier's Method vs FEM

### Use Navier's Method When:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="You Have Simply-Supported Boundaries">
    Navier's method is specifically designed for rectangular domains with simply-supported edges on all sides.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="You Need Fast, Accurate Solutions">
    Semi-analytic solutions are significantly faster than FEM for comparable accuracy, especially when evaluating results at many points.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="You're Doing Verification or Education">
    The closed-form nature makes it ideal for:
    - Verifying FEM results
    - Understanding structural behavior
    - Teaching plate and beam theory
    - Publishing benchmark solutions
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="You Have Regular Geometry">
    Rectangular plates or straight beams with constant properties are ideal candidates.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Use FEM When:

<Warning>
Navier's method has important limitations:
- **Boundary conditions**: Only simply-supported edges
- **Geometry**: Only rectangular plates or straight beams
- **Material properties**: Must be constant (no variation)
- **Connections**: Cannot model joints or connections between elements
</Warning>

For clamped edges, free edges, arbitrary shapes, or material discontinuities, use finite element methods instead.

## Solution Process

The library follows this workflow:

```mermaid
graph LR
    A[Define Geometry] --> B[Define Loads]
    B --> C[Compute Load Coefficients]
    C --> D[Assemble Stiffness Matrix]
    D --> E[Solve for Fourier Coefficients]
    E --> F[Evaluate Results at Points]
```

1. **Define the problem geometry** using `NavierBeam` or `NavierPlate`
2. **Define loads** using `PointLoad`, `LineLoad`, `RectangleLoad`, or `DiskLoad`
3. **Compute load coefficients** in Fourier space (automatic)
4. **Assemble the stiffness matrix** based on the mechanical model
5. **Solve the linear system** for Fourier coefficients
6. **Evaluate results** (displacement, rotation, moment, shear, stress) at any points

## Example: Simple Beam Problem

```python
from sigmaepsilon.solid.fourier import NavierBeam, PointLoad

# Define an Euler-Bernoulli beam
beam = NavierBeam(
    length=10.0,      # 10 meter beam
    N=100,            # 100 harmonic terms
    EI=2000.0         # Bending stiffness
)

# Apply a point load at mid-span
load = PointLoad(
    domain=5.0,       # At x = 5.0 m
    value=[1000.0, 0.0]  # [Force, Moment]
)

# Solve and get results
results = beam.linear_static_analysis(load, points=[2.5, 5.0, 7.5])

# Access displacement
print(results['w'])  # Vertical displacement at the points
```
